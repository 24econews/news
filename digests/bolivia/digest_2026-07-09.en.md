The official dollar exchange rate broke through 10 bolivianos for the first time today, a psychological threshold that crystallizes with brutal clarity the scale of Bolivia's macroeconomic deterioration and reframes every other piece of news from the day as symptoms of a single systemic illness.

The dollar's crossing of that threshold is no minor technical footnote. For more than a decade Bolivia maintained a fixed exchange rate of 6.96 bolivianos per dollar, a monetary anchor that Evo Morales's government turned into a symbol of stability and that survived, with growing artificiality, until international reserves evaporated. The transition toward a more flexible regime, which in theory was meant to bring order to the FX market, has instead produced an accelerated depreciation that erodes household purchasing power and makes imports more expensive in a country that depends on them for fuel, processed food and industrial inputs. Crossing Bs 10 is not a destination; it is, at best, a way station in an adjustment whose endpoint no one in La Paz appears in a position to define.

The paradox exposed by the FX print is enormous. Between January and May Bolivia posted a trade surplus of USD 1.393 billion, according to official figures cited by El Deber, which in any conventional economy should support the currency. That the boliviano is depreciating sharply while the trade balance is positive indicates the problem is not one of current flows but of confidence, capital flight, and hard-currency demand that the formal system cannot satisfy. The parallel market continues to trade at significant spreads over the official rate, and the FX gap functions as an implicit tax on legal productive activity.

Against that backdrop, the fuel crisis darkens the outlook further. The government officially acknowledged that diesel and gasoline shortages will extend at least through the end of July, a horizon that the transport sector received with barely contained outrage. Drivers' unions gave the executive a 48-hour ultimatum to come to the negotiating table, forcefully demanding that the government "tell the truth" about the origin and duration of the problem, according to Los Tiempos. The fuel shortage is not merely a logistical inconvenience: it has direct effects on food transport costs, on agricultural productivity in the east, and on imported inflation that the official data themselves are trying to play down. El Deber noted today, with notable statistical irony, that inflation is "in retreat" while the real economy "is in retreat too," a headline that captures the dissociation between formal indicators and the day-to-day experience of firms and consumers.

The government's response to the energy crisis runs through attracting foreign capital into the hydrocarbons sector. The relevant minister announced the drafting of a new legal framework for the sector, a signal aimed squarely at Petrobras. Bolivia and Brazil agreed this week to set up technical working groups to expand the Brazilian state oil company's footprint on Bolivian territory, according to El Deber. Petrobras, whose ADRs trade on the New York Stock Exchange, already operates in Bolivia and knows the country's regulatory complexities well. The key will be whether the new legal framework offers sufficient guarantees on profit repatriation, dispute resolution and fiscal stability — conditions that MAS governments systematically complicated after the 2006 nationalization. Without those guarantees, the technical working groups will amount to diplomacy without consequences.

On the forestry front, the Cámara Forestal reported a 66% drop in sector exports, a figure that reflects both weak external demand and the effects of prior-year wildfires, difficulty accessing credit, and logistical costs stemming precisely from the fuel shortage. Bolivia's forestry sector, one of the few with diversified export potential, is losing ground in markets that will not wait.

CAF — Banco de Desarrollo de América Latina — announced a package of more than USD 400 million to accompany President Paz's administration on infrastructure and public works, offering some external financial relief. In parallel, Bolivia received more than USD 400 million in remittances during the first half of the year, with Spain contributing roughly half, according to Bolsamania. Both flows are genuine oxygen, but they neither substitute for productive investment nor resolve the structural dependence on hydrocarbon revenues.

The convergence of those three fault lines will determine whether the coming weeks bring a tactical stabilization or a new phase of deterioration.
