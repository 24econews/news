Bolivia on Monday abandoned a fixed exchange rate that had held for nearly fifteen years, ushering in a flexible regime laden with urgency but also uncertainty, amid an economy battered by road blockades, foreign currency shortages, and an external debt that has already reached $14.131 billion.

The Banco Central de Bolivia set the dollar's initial rate at Bs 9.73 on Monday, a figure that edged up to Bs 9.76 the following day, while the parallel market traded around Bs 10, according to El Deber. The gap between the two quotes, though still moderate, reveals that the banking system opened without a functioning supply of foreign currency, laying bare from day one the structural limitations that prompted the regime change. The BCB warned it would ensure the fluctuation did not reach "extremes" and reserved the right to intervene. Economy Minister José Gabriel Espinoza sought to calm market anxiety by insisting that "practically nothing" would change for ordinary citizens, arguing that 99.3% of credit is denominated in bolivianos. On the same day, Bolivia reported turnover of just $17 million on the first day of the new scheme, a sign that foreign currency liquidity remains scarce.

The government of President Rodrigo Paz responded with a broad package of offsetting measures. It cut import tariffs by 5% to cushion the price increases that the boliviano's depreciation will bring, opened the fuel market to private players through 2030, and liberalized the export and import of electricity. It also authorized, starting July 15, the return of dollar deposits between $1,000 and $3,000 that had been trapped in the financial system, and launched a financial relief program allowing debt rescheduling. The tax authority extended until July 10 the deadline for installment payments, acknowledging the direct impact of the blockades on the liquidity of thousands of taxpayers. Espinoza also ruled out any new increase in fuel prices this year, seeking to politically shield an adjustment that Public Works Minister Mauricio Zamora openly conceded is eroding President Paz's popularity.

The political opposition was quick to react. Former president Evo Morales branded the new regime a "disguised devaluation," while economist Lara warned that the measure will not stabilize but rather accelerate inflation. Trade guilds, for their part, deemed the shift insufficient and demanded additional stimulus measures. The Instituto Boliviano de Comercio Exterior (IBCE) struck a more nuanced tone, acknowledging that the flexible exchange rate "brings the economy in line with reality" but noting that the real challenge now is attracting more foreign currency into the country.

That debate is unfolding over an economy whose most immediate wounds stem from 47 to 50 days of road blockades that, according to the government's own figures, caused damages exceeding $2.8 billion. The agricultural sector—with soy, poultry, dairy, and banana producers among the hardest hit—also faces a diesel shortage that threatens the harvest. The president of YPFB pledged to normalize fuel distribution by Tuesday, though he attributed the delays to new quality controls. Construction firms warned that the depreciation will drive up the cost of both public and private works, and Espinoza himself acknowledged that growth could turn negative in 2026 if the accumulated damage from the conflict is not reversed. The industrial sector had already been contracting since 2023, with hydrocarbons—the backbone of the previous economic model—collapsing by 13.4%.
