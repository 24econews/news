According to ABC Color, a depreciation of the guaraní would have direct consequences for public accounts, given that a significant portion of Paraguay's sovereign debt is denominated in dollars and spending on energy imports and intermediate goods is structurally elevated. The paradox is that a depreciation, while harmful to debt service, could momentarily ease the competitive position of agro-industrial exports, particularly soy and beef, which form the core of Paraguay's export model.

The Dirección Nacional de Ingresos Tributarios and representatives of the accounting sector agree that there is room to increase revenue without raising tax rates, betting instead on improved collection efficiency and reduced evasion. It is an argument that has echoed across Latin America for years, but in Paraguay's case it takes on particular relevance given that the country maintains one of the lowest tax burdens in the region — the Personal Income Tax, introduced less than a decade ago, remains politically sensitive. If the new package of seven economic laws addresses this dimension, its potential impact on public finances could be considerable, though recent legislative history counsels caution about implementation timelines.
