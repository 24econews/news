# Uruguay: The Economic Pulse — 2026-07-21

The Consejo Fiscal Autónomo warned Parliament this week that the Ministry of Economy has overestimated its growth projections in the Rendición de Cuentas — a technical critique that lands at the least convenient moment for the Frente Amplio government: precisely as Minister Gabriel Oddone attempts to persuade opposition legislators to approve a budget requesting an additional USD 1 billion in spending, and while the Coalición Republicana has announced it will not vote in favor of the document. The tension between the government's fiscal ambitions and the credibility of its macroeconomic numbers is now the central axis of Uruguay's economic conversation.

The situation has layers of complexity. On one hand, Oddone has publicly defended the revisions to the growth figures, arguing that "projection errors or deviations are commonplace" in any administration. He has a point in technical terms: the Uruguayan economy grew a mere 1.8% in 2025, below official forecasts, and closed the year with two consecutive quarters of contraction that led some analysts to speak of a "technical recession." The first quarter of 2026 brought partial relief, with an expansion of between 0.8% and 0.9% versus the previous quarter, but the minister himself has acknowledged that there is "a fairly high probability" of revising full-year projections downward. The World Bank has already trimmed its estimates for Uruguay to 1.6% for 2026 and 1.7% for 2027, while the leading economic activity index from think tank CERES has flashed mixed signals: after months of weakening, it posted a 0.2% increase in April, though the institution warns that the economy "began 2026 decelerating and without clear signs of growth."

On the other hand, the government is betting that private investment will offset moderating consumption and fiscal constraints. According to El Observador, the Ministry of Economy is banking on three large blocks of private works to drive activity: green hydrogen projects, the installation of data centers, and the expansion of the pulp industry. The first ties Uruguay to the global energy transition and to growing institutional interest in green assets; the second reflects the country's consolidation as a regional tech platform, a narrative reinforced by the recent record in Knowledge Economy services exports; the third is the continuation of a pulp investment cycle that has been a structural engine of the Uruguayan economy for two decades.

Layered onto these domestic vectors is a piece of regional energy geopolitics with direct implications for state oil company Ancap. Crude from Vaca Muerta, the shale formation in Argentine Patagonia, is consolidating itself as a key supply source for the Uruguayan refinery, progressively displacing other origins. The logic is clear: energy integration with Argentina lowers transport costs and taps into YPF's growing production surplus. Nevertheless, this dependence introduces a geopolitical concentration risk at a time when regional relations remain volatile and when Minister Oddone himself has flagged the conflict in the Middle East as a potential source of a "negative shock" to energy prices.

On the financial front, the Banco Central presented a draft bill to create an open finance system, a signal of modernization that aligns Uruguay with regulatory trends in Europe and in the region's more developed markets. The financial system remains solid: the Comité de Estabilidad Financiera stated that it "is well prepared to absorb hypothetical adverse scenarios," an institutional endorsement of some relevance in an external environment that the government itself describes as "challenging and uncertain." Meanwhile, the Banco Central and the MEF returned to the market this week to place local debt worth more than USD 33 million, a liability management operation that complements recent global bond issuances.

Household disposable income offers an encouraging, if modest, signal: the money left after covering fixed expenses stopped falling after seven consecutive months of decline, potentially anticipating a gradual recovery in private consumption. However, a survey by consultancy Cifra warns that the economic climate as perceived by Uruguayans "has been deteriorating" — a divergence between macro data and public perception that the government will need to manage in parallel with the budget negotiation.

In the coming days, markets and analysts will be tracking three simultaneous vectors: the evolution of parliamentary negotiations around the Rendición de Cuentas, where Oddone has said he "will not give up negotiating"; the release of fresh activity data that will either confirm or undercut the first-quarter rebound; and any further signals on the timing and scope of reforms to the AFAP regime, which the government pledged to advance within the framework of the Diálogo Social and which the private sector is watching with growing caution.
