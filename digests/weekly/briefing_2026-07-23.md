# Mercosur Weekly Briefing — Week of July 20–23, 2026

## The Big Picture

Two forces dominated the Mercosur region this week, and their interaction will define the investment calculus for months to come. The first is a genuine, if uneven, wave of financial credibility: Argentina secured a historic three-agency rating alignment at B- — Moody's, S&P, and Fitch converging for the first time in over a decade — while Paraguay's investment grade was reaffirmed and Bolivia struck a framework agreement with the IMF worth up to $2.8 billion. The second is an exogenous shock of the kind that exposes every fiscal seam: Brent crude surging toward $100 a barrel on Houthi attacks against Saudi tankers and the threatened closure of the Strait of Hormuz, repricing energy costs, lifting U.S. long rates, and forcing governments across the region to choose between absorbing the blow or passing it to consumers. The week's central paradox is that the two forces arrived simultaneously — just as Mercosur's reform stories were gaining institutional credibility, an oil shock reminded markets how thin the margin for error remains.

The divergence between financial and real-economy signals is the thread that connects the region's six economies this week. In Argentina, country risk broke below 410 basis points and bank ADRs surged 5% on Wall Street even as May's economic activity index posted its second consecutive monthly decline and registered wages fell behind inflation for the fifth straight month. In Brazil, the Ibovespa closed at 177,547 points and the real firmed to R$5.05 — while NTN-B real yields above 8% across virtually every maturity through 2037 signaled a bond market that has not been convinced by the same rally. In Chile, an 80-to-48 vote cleared the final legislative hurdle on a landmark corporate tax reform reducing the first-category rate from 27% to 23%, even as the government faced an immediate decision on whether to activate its gasoline stabilization mechanism against a 3.4% single-day surge in Brent. Bolivia's exchange-rate adjustment — the dollar touching Bs 11 in the flexible market after fifteen years at Bs 6.96 — is the starkest illustration of what deferred adjustment costs: a repricing so abrupt that construction firms are reporting unviable contracts and 800,000 tons of soybeans in Santa Cruz face harvest risk amid a simultaneous diesel shortage.

For global investors, the actionable takeaway is tiered. Argentina's rating convergence is a structural event, not a sentiment spike: mandates requiring two or three aligned ratings now have a technical green light to allocate to Argentine assets, and the CAF's approval of a $250 million sovereign guarantee confirms that the external financing architecture for 2026–2027 debt maturities is largely in place. The risk is that the real economy — where investment as a share of GDP has fallen to 14% from 16% in just one year, against a sustainability threshold of roughly 25% — does not validate the financial narrative fast enough. Brazil's story remains the region's most consequential and most uncomfortable: gross public debt at 81.1% of GDP, 25% U.S. tariffs in force, and a sovereign now paying real yields that Santander analysts describe as without precedent in 18 years of NTN-B coverage. The Ibovespa rally is real, but it is running on earnings momentum and commodity prices — not fiscal consolidation. Chile and Uruguay, meanwhile, represent the region's more stable end of the spectrum, though neither is insulated: Chile's SQM choosing to invest $1 billion in Australian lithium rather than expand at the Salar de Atacama is a quiet verdict on domestic regulatory risk that Boric's successor will inherit.

## Country by Country

### 🇧🇷 Brazil
Brent's approach toward $100 a barrel — driven by Houthi attacks on Saudi tankers and mounting Strait of Hormuz tensions — arrived at the worst possible moment for Brazil's fiscal arithmetic, reopening the diesel subsidy debate around Petrobras (NYSE: PBR) while simultaneously pressuring global rates: the 30-year U.S. Treasury traded at 5.16%, near its highest level since 2007, intensifying stress on NTN-Bs where real yields above 8% now span every maturity through 2037. The Ibovespa closed at 177,547 points on Wednesday — up 2.43% — and the dollar retreated to R$5.05, boosted by commodity tailwinds and the start of Q2 earnings season, but the rally coexists uneasily with gross public debt at 81.1% of GDP, 25% U.S. tariffs on Brazilian exports now in force, and a R$18.5 billion stimulus package (Brasil Soberano 3) that economists compare unfavorably to the post-2008 programs that preceded the 2015–2016 crisis. Vale's (NYSE: VALE) boardroom drama — shareholders electing Previ-backed "Ollie" as chairman while the board removed Marcelo Gasparino for leaking confidential information — and Nubank's (NYSE: NU) acquisition of Banco Porto Real to satisfy Central Bank licensing requirements were the week's defining corporate episodes, the latter also notable for David Vélez joining OpenAI's board of directors.

### 🇦🇷 Argentina
The alignment of all three major rating agencies at B- for the first time in more than a decade sent the S&P Merval to a nominal record of 3,379,772 points and pushed country risk to the edge of 410 basis points — a level last seen in 2018 — while Moody's positive outlook leaves the door open to a further upgrade within six to eighteen months and Daza set an explicit target of 300 basis points for sovereign spreads. The financial fireworks, however, obscure a deteriorating real economy: the May EMAE posted its second consecutive monthly decline, investment as a share of GDP fell to 14% from 16% in just one year, registered wages lost 4.4% of purchasing power since January 2025, and mass consumption in June fell 2.7% year-on-year — sitting 17 points below its December 2023 peak. The counterweight is Vaca Muerta: a record first-half trade surplus of $13.923 billion — five times the year-ago figure — and new Energy Secretariat Resolution 166/2026 eliminating mandatory domestic-market hydrocarbon supply requirements ahead of export, a deregulatory move U.S. Deputy Assistant Secretary Caleb Orr called "miraculous" at the Atlantic Council.

### 🇨🇱 Chile
Chile's Chamber of Deputies approved the joint committee report on the tax reform by 80 votes to 48, clearing the final legislative obstacle and setting up Senate enactment of a bill that cuts the first-category corporate tax from 27% to 23% through 2029 and restores full tax integration — reversing a decade of the semi-integrated system introduced under Bachelet. SOFOFA projects the reform will generate at least 80,800 additional direct jobs and roughly $6.7 billion in incremental investment over 2026–2030, moving Chile from 11th to 16th in the OECD corporate tax ranking, on par with Israel and Austria. The reform's passage coincided with Brent's 3.36% single-day surge to $94.07, forcing Finance Minister Quiroz into an immediate decision on MEPCO activation — without it, gasoline prices could rise roughly 30 pesos next week — while SQM's decision to invest up to $1 billion in doubling spodumene output at its Australian Mt Holland deposit, rather than expanding at the Salar de Atacama, delivered a pointed signal about the domestic regulatory environment for lithium.

### 🇺🇾 Uruguay
Uruguay's Autonomous Fiscal Council told Parliament this week that the growth projections embedded in the government's Rendición de Cuentas are overstated — a rare piece of institutional candor that landed while Economy Minister Gabriel Oddone was already publicly acknowledging a "fairly high probability" of downward revisions to 2026 forecasts. The World Bank has cut its Uruguay projections to 1.6% for 2026 and 1.7% for 2027, the Central Bank's own estimates trail the MEF's, and private analysts are pointing to a year that effectively begins flat, with no positive statistical carryover from a 2025 that closed with a stagnant fourth quarter. The government's response — betting on capital-intensive anchor projects in green hydrogen, data centers, and pulp — is structurally sound but temporally misaligned with the political calendar, while the dollar's largest weekly gain in six years is cutting both ways: easing the exchange-rate lag that had pushed agricultural exporters to the breaking point, while introducing new inflation and wage-purchasing-power risks.

### 🇵🇾 Paraguay
Moody's reaffirmed Paraguay's investment grade this week but delivered a pointed diagnosis: sovereign risk no longer resides in the debt level but in the quality of fiscal management — and the data back that assessment. The Caja Fiscal accumulated a deficit of 1.31 trillion guaraníes ($216 million) in the first half, public debt grew by $1.343 billion in five months, and a pension reform already in progress is adding structural pressure even as the economy expands at 5.6% year-to-date through May, one of the region's strongest growth rates. New Economy Minister Óscar Lovera — replacing Carlos Fernández Valdovinos amid reported internal tensions — travels to France for meetings with international organizations, an agenda that carries heightened urgency given the gap between headline growth and fiscal revenue performance, and a congressional vote on Caja Fiscal reform is expected imminently with modifications.

### 🇧🇴 Bolivia
The dollar touched Bs 11 in Bolivia's flexible market this week — a level unthinkable two months ago when the peg still held at Bs 6.96 — as the government confirmed an IMF agreement that could mobilize up to $2.8 billion and announced the staggered return of more than $930 million in previously restricted bank deposits beginning July 15. The speed of the depreciation, not merely its magnitude, is the defining feature: construction firms are reporting financially unviable public contracts, 80% of interdepartmental buses are paralyzed by a diesel shortage that also puts 800,000 tons of Santa Cruz soybeans at harvest risk, and informal dollarization is already spreading as suppliers refuse bolivianos in some commercial transactions. Economy Minister Espinoza has pledged the rate will stabilize below Bs 11, with 99.3% of financial system loans denominated in bolivianos providing a structural buffer against balance-sheet deterioration — but Fitch maintains a negative outlook, and the credibility of the entire transition rests on whether the exchange rate converges as promised or the gap between official rhetoric and market price continues to widen.

## Corporate Watch of the Week

**Vale (NYSE: VALE) — Brazil:** The world's largest iron ore exporter concluded a bruising boardroom succession this week with shareholders electing Previ-backed Manuel Lino Silva de Sousa Oliveira ("Ollie") as chairman, immediately followed by the board's own vote to remove rival candidate Marcelo Gasparino after an independent outside investigation confirmed the leaking of confidential information from a June board meeting. The episode is significant beyond governance optics: Vale is navigating a commodity-price cycle heavily influenced by Chinese steel demand at precisely the moment its leadership transition introduced maximum uncertainty for institutional shareholders.

**Nubank (NYSE: NU) — Brazil:** The fintech announced the acquisition of 100% of Banco Porto Real de Investimentos, securing the banking license required under Central Bank Joint Resolution 17 and elegantly resolving a regulatory overhang that had threatened Nubank's use of the "bank" brand across its Latin American operations. The deal was announced the same week CEO David Vélez joined OpenAI's board, a pairing that underscores the company's ambitions well beyond digital banking.

**SQM (NYSE: SQM) — Chile:** The lithium miner confirmed it will invest up to $1 billion alongside Australian partner Wesfarmers to double spodumene concentrate production at the Mt Holland deposit in Western Australia. The decision — driven by recovering lithium prices and regulatory and environmental constraints at the Salar de Atacama — represents the clearest market signal yet that Chile's flagship critical mineral company is structuring its next growth phase outside Chilean territory, a strategic verdict the incoming government cannot afford to ignore.

## What to Watch Next Week

- **Argentina's country risk trajectory and reserve accumulation data:** With Moody's positive outlook leaving a further upgrade possible within six to eighteen months, and Fitch identifying reserves as the decisive 2027 variable, the Central Bank's weekly reserve figures will be the most closely watched data point in the region — particularly as Brent near $100 tests YPF's (NYSE: YPF) export break-even and the Fiscal Innocence bill's revised text enters congressional debate.
- **Brazil's tariff negotiations with Washington and Q2 earnings releases:** The U.S. government may still impose an additional tariff arising from a forced-labor-in-third-markets investigation, and the outcome of that process — alongside second-quarter results from Petrobras, Vale, and the major banks — will determine whether the Ibovespa rally can sustain itself against the headwind of NTN-B yields above 8% and a fiscal narrative the bond market has yet to endorse.
- **Bolivia's exchange rate stabilization and the IMF program timeline:** Minister Espinoza's pledge that the dollar will hold below Bs 11 faces its first real test as deposit returns begin and informal dollarization spreads; any sustained breach of that threshold, combined with a diesel shortage still threatening the Santa Cruz soy harvest, would materially complicate the government's ability to present the IMF transition as an orderly adjustment rather than a managed crisis.
