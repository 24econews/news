# Mercosur Weekly Briefing — Week of July 6, 2026 to July 9, 2026

## The Big Picture

The defining thread running through Mercosur this week was not growth — it was the price of holding things together. Bolivia finally surrendered a peg that had been fictional for years, watching the boliviano breach Bs 10 to the dollar within days of its "flexible" debut, while inflation running at 9.23% and $2.8 billion in blockade-related damages left the new regime with almost no cushion for error. At the other end of the spectrum, Argentina celebrated gross reserves touching $49.5 billion — their highest since 2019 — but the engineering behind that number tells a more fragile story: multilateral guarantees from the World Bank and IDB underwrote the private bank lending that made the $4.4 billion bondholder payment possible, and JP Morgan was direct in flagging that the real test arrives in 2027, when maturities near $25 billion and market access remains, in Caputo's own words, an option rather than a plan. Both countries are, in different ways, purchasing time with borrowed credibility.

The Middle East war inserted itself into the region's fiscal calendars with unusual force. Brazil's finance minister was forced to postpone the final removal of the gasoline subsidy — a R$0.44-per-liter decision that markets had priced in — because Brent pushing back toward $80 made the political arithmetic untenable. Russia's drone-hit refineries cut diesel exports, threatening the supply chain of the world's second-largest importer of that derivative. Chile watched Brent spike more than 8% in a single session, complicating any projection of sustained fuel-price relief and adding a new variable to an inflation print that already came in above consensus at 4.3% annual. For Paraguay, higher oil prices arrive at a moment when the Caja Fiscal is already running a $182 million deficit through May and consumer purchasing power is visibly eroding despite a headline GDP growth rate of 6.6% in 2024 — a gap between macro optics and lived experience that the country's business community is no longer willing to politely ignore.

The IMF's updated World Economic Outlook cut the global growth forecast to 3.0% and pushed world inflation to 4.7%, but the Fund's regional scorecard was surprisingly divergent. Brazil received the second-largest upward revision of any country globally, to 2.4%, while Argentina's 3.5% projection held unchanged. Uruguay, by contrast, is staring at sub-1% quarterly growth and a consumer confidence reading at its weakest in three years, even as its country risk touches 2018 lows — a split screen that captures the broader Mercosur paradox this week: financial markets pricing in stability while the real economy signals something considerably more complicated. For global investors, the actionable read is this: the region's macro architecture is more resilient than its headlines suggest, but the political economy of sustaining that architecture — fuel subsidies in Brazil, reserve engineering in Argentina, pension reform in Paraguay, an EV tax that fractured Uruguay's cabinet — is growing more expensive by the week.

## Country by Country

### 🇧🇷 Brazil
Iran's war premium forced Finance Minister Dario Durigan to shelve the planned removal of Brazil's R$0.44-per-liter gasoline subsidy, turning what was intended as a clean fiscal normalization into an exercise in reactive delay — the announcement will now come "partially or fully" next week, contingent on crude behavior. The IMF's upgrade of Brazil's 2026 growth forecast to 2.4%, the second-largest upward revision globally, arrived simultaneously with the Treasury warning that tax-exempt securities are distorting demand for conventional government bonds and raising sovereign funding costs at precisely the wrong moment. With the July 15 deadline for Washington's final tariff decision looming — a proposed 25% levy whose collateral damage is already visible in US-bound exports falling to their lowest share since 1997 — Brazil is simultaneously managing a Middle East shock, a rural debt renegotiation worth up to R$100 billion, and the early stages of a yuan-denominated panda bond program designed to reduce dependence on New York and European capital markets.

### 🇦🇷 Argentina
Argentina's $4.4 billion bondholder payment went smoothly on paper, but its architecture — BBVA, Santander, and Deutsche Bank lending against World Bank and IDB guarantees at roughly 6.3% — confirms that Buenos Aires is accessing multilateral credibility as a substitute for sovereign market access, not a supplement to it. The 2026 financial program shows a $3.7 billion cushion, but JP Morgan's client note was pointed: 2027's near-$25 billion maturity wall depends on full rollover of local-law dollar bonds, sustained multilateral flows, and a return to international markets that Caputo himself frames as optional. Meanwhile, May's industrial production fell 5.7% year-on-year — 14 of 16 divisions in contraction, with household appliances down 34.1% and automobiles off 15.9% — while oil production exceeded 900,000 barrels per day for the first time in Argentine history and lithium output is up 47.5%, sketching the outlines of an economy that is healing unevenly and at an uncomfortable speed for the workers caught on the wrong side of that divide.

### 🇨🇱 Chile
The PPD's deal with Finance Minister Jorge Quiroz on tax invariability clauses — reducing the original 25-year horizon to ten, fifteen, and twenty years depending on investment size — fractured the center-left opposition and handed the Kast administration the legal certainty for investors it needed, at the cost of a visible coalition rupture that the government will need to manage carefully. The political win arrived against a difficult macro backdrop: the IMF warned that Chilean public debt will breach its own prudential 45%-of-GDP ceiling in 2028 absent additional fiscal measures worth roughly one point of GDP, while June CPI printed at 0% against an expected negative reading, pushing annual inflation to 4.3% and effectively closing the door on Monetary Policy Rate cuts for the remainder of 2026. The electrical system is operating under emergency conditions — generators formally requested a preventive rationing decree — and the July 24 USTR hearing, where Chile faces the highest proposed punitive tariff of any economy in the process at 12.5%, remains unresolved.

### 🇺🇾 Uruguay
Uruguay's country risk is at its lowest since 2018, OECD membership talks have formally begun, and knowledge economy exports are posting record growth — and yet the economy expanded just 0.8% quarter-on-quarter in Q1 2026, consumers are at their most pessimistic in three years, and June inflation broke above 4% annually after touching seven-decade lows, reversing a trend the Central Bank had worked hard to establish. Economy Minister Gabriel Oddone's Rendición de Cuentas attempts to square that circle by raising spending within fiscal targets, funded in part by a reinstatement of the IMESI tax on electric vehicles above $19,000 in import value — a measure that prompted the Ministers of Environment and Industry to publicly distance themselves from Economy, an unusual fracture for an administration only months into its term. The gap between Uruguay's financial credibility, priced by markets, and the competitiveness concerns voiced by virtually every business sector surveyed this week is the tension that will define the government's second semester.

### 🇵🇾 Paraguay
Paraguay's headline numbers remain among the region's strongest — 6.6% growth in 2024, 5.8% in Q1 2026, IMF forecast at 4.4% for 2025 — but the week's most revealing signal came from the business community's measured rebuttal of President Peña's annual address: construction and SME leaders praised the macro while demanding faster resolution of a $340 million contractor debt and a structural increase in infrastructure investment that the Capaco chamber said needs to triple. The Caja Fiscal's $182 million deficit through May, interest payments growing at double-digit rates, and a credit portfolio where consumer lending now accounts for 20% of total banking assets — flagged explicitly as a warning by a former minister — suggest that the growth story is being partly financed by fiscal and household balance sheet stress that the headline GDP figure does not capture. The reform of the Caja Fiscal, which Senate leader Blanca Alliana suggested Congress could pass this week with modifications, is the single most consequential domestic variable for Paraguay's medium-term fiscal trajectory.

### 🇧🇴 Bolivia
Bolivia's boliviano crossed Bs 10 to the dollar this week — a threshold that would have been politically unthinkable under the fifteen-year peg that the Central Bank maintained until it no longer could — and the speed of the depreciation from the Bs 9.73 launch rate confirmed what every parallel market participant already knew: the overvaluation was substantial, and the adjustment has only begun. The devaluation is landing on an economy simultaneously absorbing 9.23% inflation, $2.8 billion in blockade-related damages, a fuel shortage that YPFB acknowledges will extend to late July, and losses at state-owned enterprises tallying Bs 4 billion over sixteen years — a structural backdrop that leaves almost no room for the new flexible regime to settle cleanly. The imminent publication of YPFB's gas reserves report is the single most consequential near-term data release: if it confirms the most pessimistic production-decline estimates, it will remove the commodity underpinning that Bolivia's government has used for two decades to justify its state-led economic model.

## Corporate Watch of the Week

**Vale (NYSE: VALE)** — Brazil's CVM opened proceedings to investigate a possible irregularity in Daniel Stieler's resignation from the chair of Vale's board, under pressure from Previ, the Banco do Brasil pension fund. The probe — touching the world's largest iron ore producer at a moment when its corporate governance is already under investor scrutiny — adds regulatory risk to a stock whose performance is already sensitive to Chinese demand signals and geopolitical volatility.

**LATAM Airlines (Santiago: LTM)** — The carrier called an extraordinary shareholders' meeting for August 3 to approve a buyback of up to 5% of its own stock, extendable over five years. Having executed two similar programs totaling $585 million last year, LATAM is signaling confidence in its balance sheet recovery at a moment when Latin American aviation demand remains structurally robust but Middle East tensions are beginning to pressure jet fuel costs.

**Petrobras (NYSE: PBR; B3: PETR4)** — Bolivia and Brazil agreed to establish technical working groups to expand Petrobras's role in the Bolivian energy sector, in a context where YPFB is grappling with declining gas reserves and La Paz is opening hydrocarbons to private operators through 2030. For Petrobras, the agreement represents a potential avenue for upstream expansion in a neighbor whose infrastructure gaps are significant — and whose desperation for foreign investment is, at this particular moment, at its highest in years.

## What to Watch Next Week

- **Brazil's fuel subsidy decision and US tariff deadline (July 15):** Durigan's postponed call on the R$0.44-per-liter gasoline subsidy will land next week, with crude behavior as the deciding variable; simultaneously, USTR Jamieson Greer has signaled a "very soon" announcement on the proposed 25% tariff on Brazilian exports — the outcome will be the single most significant bilateral trade development for the region's largest economy in years.

- **YPFB gas reserves report, Bolivia:** The publication of Bolivia's official natural gas reserves data is the region's highest-stakes macro release of the coming days — a significant downward revision would undermine the credibility of the new flexible exchange rate regime and accelerate pressure on a government simultaneously managing a fuel crisis, transport union ultimatums, and civic committee mobilizations.

- **Paraguay's Caja Fiscal reform vote and Argentina's IMF visit:** Senate leader Alliana's indication that Paraguay's pension reform bill could clear Congress this week makes it the most consequential near-term fiscal event in Asunción; in Buenos Aires, IMF Managing Director Kristalina Georgieva's scheduled late-July visit is already being priced by markets as a confidence anchor — any signal of friction with the Fund ahead of that visit would move Argentine sovereign spreads immediately.
