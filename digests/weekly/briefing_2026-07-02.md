# Mercosur Weekly Briefing — Week of June 29 to July 2, 2026

## The Big Picture

Two forces defined the Mercosur region this week, and they pull in opposite directions. The first is a wave of structural transformation — exchange-rate regimes abandoned, export duty frameworks overhauled, investment pipelines deepened — that signals governments across the bloc are making consequential bets on reform. Bolivia ended a fifteen-year currency peg, Argentina eliminated export duties on nearly a thousand industrial product lines and pushed RIGI-committed investment past USD 46 billion, and Brazil quietly elevated critical minerals policy to the core of the executive branch. The second force is the accumulating cost of those bets: fiscal slippage, currency volatility, and credit deterioration that are eroding the cushion available to absorb shocks. Brazil's non-performing loan ratio hit 4.7%, a record since the Central Bank began the series in 2011. Argentina's household delinquency reached 12.7% for the nineteenth consecutive month, leaving roughly seven million people outside the credit system. Bolivia's private sector estimates losses exceeding USD 2.8 billion from fifty days of road blockades. Optimism about the region's medium-term trajectory is warranted; confidence in the near-term execution is not.

The commodity picture adds texture to that tension. Argentina recorded an all-time oil production record of 903,700 barrels per day in May, with Vaca Muerta accounting for 69% of national output — a structural shift, not a blip. Brazil's Petrobras posted the second-largest monthly oil volume in its history at 4.3 million barrels per day. Yet Chile's Codelco publicly acknowledged it has lost its position as the world's largest copper producer, with costs running 57% above competitors, even as BHP, KGHM and South32 collectively committed more than USD 2.2 billion to new Chilean mining capacity. The region is resource-rich and reform-active; it is also fiscally strained and politically complex in ways that demand granular due diligence rather than blanket confidence.

The geopolitical dimension sharpens the picture further. The U.S. designation of Brazil's PCC and Comando Vermelho as terrorist organizations introduced a new layer of compliance risk for Brazilian financial institutions, with Febraban acknowledging it cannot identify precise exposures without a Treasury target list. A July 15 deadline looms for Washington to confirm or suspend 25% tariffs on Brazilian products under Section 301. Uruguay is fielding daily U.S. pressure to curtail trade ties with China. And Chile's foreign minister is heading to Washington amid renewed tariff threats, even as speculative short positions against the peso reach historic levels. Mercosur is not insulated from the global trade conflict — it is increasingly a front line in it.

## Country by Country

### 🇧🇷 Brazil
Brazil's fiscal architecture showed cracks on multiple fronts simultaneously: the delayed implementation of the Selective Tax threatens a R$10 billion revenue hole in early 2027, state debt renegotiations will cost the National Treasury R$347 billion over 30 years, and the U.S. designation of the PCC as a terrorist organization created unquantifiable compliance exposure for the banking system. The real fell nearly 3% in June — its worst monthly performance of the year — while the formal labor market added just 72,900 jobs in May, the weakest result for the month since the pandemic. Against that backdrop, Petrobras's record-adjacent oil output and the R$1.8 billion restart of the Três Lagoas fertilizer plant offer genuine bright spots, but they cannot mask the fact that Brazil's risk premium is back at the center of institutional portfolio conversations.

### 🇦🇷 Argentina
Argentina's country risk touched an eight-year low of 421 basis points, a number that would have seemed implausible two years ago and that reflects real fiscal discipline — a primary surplus sustained even as the government absorbed the USD 80 million cost of eliminating export duties on close to a thousand industrial products via Decree 566/2026. The harder question is durability: June tax collection fell roughly 7% in real terms, agri-sector foreign-exchange settlements dropped 13% in the first half versus 2025, and a USD 4.2 billion Bonares payment falls due in mid-July. The BCRA's accumulation of more than USD 11.1 billion in reserves this year provides meaningful buffer, but the post-harvest seasonality and the gap between the wholesale rate and the band ceiling at around $1,844 will test the floating regime's credibility in the weeks ahead.

### 🇨🇱 Chile
Chile recorded its fifth consecutive monthly contraction in economic activity, with the May Imacec falling 0.9% year-on-year — a streak without precedent since the 2009 subprime crisis — as mining output collapsed 11.6% annually and single-handedly subtracted 1.5 percentage points from the headline. National unemployment settled at 9.4%, with youth joblessness reaching 22.8%, while Codelco publicly acknowledged it has lost its standing as the world's largest copper producer. Finance Minister Quiroz's decision to join Senate-convened fiscal reform negotiations signals that political pressure is finally translating into policy motion, but the June Imacec print — expected in coming weeks — will determine whether Chile formally enters technical recession and accelerate the Central Bank's hand on rate cuts.

### 🇺🇾 Uruguay
Uruguay's Economy Minister Gabriel Oddone submitted a Rendición de Cuentas to Parliament that increases spending by just USD 31 million — disciplined by any regional standard — while acknowledging a "fairly high probability" of revising the 2026 growth forecast downward from an already modest base. GDP expanded 0.8% in Q1 quarter-on-quarter, driven by private consumption and services exports, but household credit has contracted for seven consecutive months and the World Bank projects only 1.6% growth for the full year. The OECD membership roadmap and the 240-measure Competitiveness and Cost-of-Living Reduction Bill signal structural ambition; the gap between that ambition and near-term demand dynamics is Uruguay's defining tension heading into the second half.
Link: https://24econews.com/uruguay/2026-07-02

### 🇵🇾 Paraguay
Paraguay's economy expanded 5.8% in Q1 and is tracking near 5% for the full year — the strongest growth rate in the region by a considerable margin — yet the fiscal architecture beneath that headline is visibly strained. Public debt grew USD 1.333 billion in just four months, interest service jumped 16.8% year-on-year, and the state owes the construction sector more than USD 300 million in arrears. The newly merged tax authority DNIT suffered a system failure on its first major filing deadline, raising questions about operational capacity precisely when the agency needs to demonstrate credibility, while the London Stock Exchange suspended Atome Energy's shares after ANDE rejected its green hydrogen project — a reversal that exposes the gap between Paraguay's investment-attraction narrative and its regulatory execution.
Link: https://24econews.com/paraguay/2026-07-02

### 🇧🇴 Bolivia
Bolivia's Banco Central de Bolivia set the boliviano at Bs 9.73 per dollar on Monday — abandoning the Bs 6.96 peg it had defended for fifteen years — in the most significant monetary policy shift in the region this week. Economy Minister Espinoza insisted the change would affect "practically nothing" for most Bolivians, but the private sector's extraordinary summit told a different story: fifty days of road blockades cost at least USD 1 billion in direct foreign-trade losses, inflation could close 2026 near 17%, and some La Paz manufacturers are already relocating operations to Santa Cruz, Peru, and Paraguay. The daily rate set by the central bank, the pace of dollar deposit releases from July 15 onward, and June inflation data are the three numbers that will tell investors whether Bolivia is stabilizing or still in free fall.

## Corporate Watch of the Week

**Petrobras (Brazil):** Oil production reached 4.3 million barrels per day in May — the second-largest monthly volume in company history — as CEO Magda Chambriard ruled out passing through international price declines to pump prices, signaling a pricing regime that prioritizes fiscal stability over consumer relief. The simultaneous 14.5% cut in jet fuel prices for July provides a direct tailwind for Gol and Latam during the high season, a rare positive for two carriers that have navigated turbulent capital markets over the past 18 months.

**BHP / KGHM-South32 (Chile):** BHP filed the environmental impact study to restart Cerro Colorado with a USD 1.5 billion investment targeting 130,000 tons of copper cathodes annually, while the KGHM-South32 consortium committed USD 725 million to expand Sierra Gorda's capacity by up to 20%. Together, these commitments represent the most significant infusion of private mining capital into Chile in years — arriving precisely as state-owned Codelco conceded competitive decline, and underscoring that international majors see the copper price trajectory as compelling enough to override Chile's current recessionary environment.

**Atome Energy / ANDE (Paraguay):** The London Stock Exchange suspended trading in Atome Energy shares after a leaked internal ANDE communication revealed the state electricity utility's rejection of the company's green hydrogen project — a development that carries a USD 1.6 billion estimated cost implication for Paraguayan consumers. The episode crystallizes the regulatory and governance risk that shadows Paraguay's otherwise impressive growth story, and will be closely watched by other foreign investors currently evaluating energy-sector commitments in the country.

## What to Watch Next Week

- **Brazil's July 15 tariff deadline:** Washington must confirm or suspend the 25% Section 301 tariffs on Brazilian exports. The outcome — combined with the U.S. June payroll report already in the pipeline — will set the tone for the real and Ibovespa sentiment into mid-month, and determine whether Brazil's Finance Ministry is forced to activate the Economic Reciprocity Law.

- **Argentina's Bonares payment and bond reinvestment flows:** Approximately USD 4.2 billion in principal and interest falls due in mid-July. The proportion reinvested in local sovereign debt will be the first real stress test of market confidence in Argentina's fiscal trajectory under the floating exchange-rate regime, and will signal whether the sovereign curve's 10%-plus first-half gain has durable support.

- **Bolivia's daily exchange-rate fixing and reserve data:** Each Banco Central de Bolivia rate-setting session is now a market event, as investors attempt to determine whether the boliviano stabilizes near Bs 9.76 or continues depreciating. June inflation figures and international reserves data, due in the coming days, will be the primary inputs for any reassessment of Bolivia's sovereign risk — currently at 485 basis points but still firmly in high-risk territory.
