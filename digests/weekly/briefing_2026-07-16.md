Mercosur is navigating a week where external shocks and internal contradictions arrived simultaneously — Washington's tariff hammer fell on Brazil just as Argentina posted its best inflation print in years, and the region's smaller economies are learning that strong growth headlines and structural fragility can coexist for only so long.

— John Dominguez, Editor

# Mercosur Weekly Briefing — Week of July 13, 2026 to July 16, 2026

## The Big Picture

The defining event of the week was Washington's decision to impose a 25% tariff on Brazilian goods, effective July 22, closing a Section 301 investigation that targeted — with considerable irony — Pix, Brazil's financial inclusion flagship, alongside ethanol policy. The measure threatens more than US$11 billion in industrial and agribusiness exports, even after a 2,100-item exemption list covering beef, orange juice, oil, and aerospace components. Brazil's Reciprocity Law has been formally invoked, retaliatory measures are being designed, and the Lula administration and the private sector are already blaming each other for the negotiating failure. For global investors, the tariff lands on an economy already under strain: inflation revised up to 5.1%, a nominal fiscal deficit hovering near 9% of GDP, NTN-B real yields above 8% struggling to find buyers, and a services sector that contracted 0.4% in May. The dollar held near R$5.078 and the Ibovespa dipped 0.35% the day of the announcement — a contained reaction that may not survive the coming sessions as the full import of the measure is priced in.

Against that backdrop, Argentina delivered the most financially encouraging week of Milei's administration to date — though the real economy is not keeping pace. June CPI printed at 1.9%, the first sub-2% reading in ten months; the central bank purchased a record US$532 million in a single session, pushing gross reserves to roughly US$48.6 billion; and a Bonar 2029 auction drew US$1.046 billion in demand at a rate of 7.99%. Country risk tightened to 404 basis points. The contrast with the underlying economy is stark: industrial capacity utilization fell to 58.4%, registered private employment has shed 329,667 jobs since November 2023, and labor informality stands at 44.2%. The financial program is performing; the productive base is not recovering at the same speed, and that divergence is the central tension investors should be pricing.

The smaller economies of the bloc illustrated, in different registers, the same regional tension between headline credibility and structural fragility. Chile's Senate approved the cornerstone of Kast's mega-reform — a corporate tax cut from 27% to 23% effective 2029 — by a margin of 26 to 24 votes, even as the Budget Office's own second-quarter report shows the structural fiscal gap does not close in any year between 2027 and 2030. Uruguay received an IMF managing director visit while its own finance minister acknowledged a downward revision to the 2026 growth forecast is "quite likely," the fiscal deficit sits at 4.1% of GDP, and the World Bank has trimmed its projection to 1.6%. Paraguay is posting 5.6% activity growth through May while quietly processing US$1.6 billion in new external credits and watching its Caja Fiscal run a five-month deficit of US$182 million. And Bolivia is absorbing, simultaneously, the first day of unrestricted dollar withdrawals from banks, a flexible exchange rate that has already depreciated more than 58% from the old peg, and a fuel crisis the government admits will last through the end of July. The regional theme is not crisis — it is the accelerating gap between reform ambition and fiscal arithmetic, playing out across six very different economies at the same time.

## Country by Country

### 🇧🇷 Brazil
The Trump administration's 25% tariff on Brazilian goods — set to take effect July 22 and covering roughly 40% of Brazil's US export basket despite a 2,100-item exemption list — is the largest external shock the Brazilian economy has faced in years, and it arrives while the fiscal deficit runs near 9% of GDP and inflation has been revised above the Central Bank's ceiling to 5.1%. Brazil has formally invoked its Reciprocity Law and is designing retaliatory measures, while the Lula administration and industrial federation Fiesp exchange public blame over the negotiating outcome. Beyond the tariff, a domestic data week featuring a 0.4% services contraction and NTN-B real yields above 8% struggling to attract sufficient buyers underscores that the macro environment was already deteriorating before Washington acted.

### 🇦🇷 Argentina
Three simultaneous data points — June CPI at 1.9%, a record US$532 million single-day reserve purchase by the central bank, and a Bonar 2029 auction that drew US$1.046 billion in demand at 7.99% — produced the strongest alignment of financial indicators for the Milei administration since the monetary program launched, tightening JP Morgan's country risk index to 404 basis points and pushing leading ADRs such as Banco Supervielle up 6.2%. The caveat is consequential: industrial capacity utilization fell to 58.4% in May, the lowest since April, registered private employment has lost 329,667 jobs since November 2023, and labor informality has reached 44.2% — a real economy that is not recovering at the pace the financial market is pricing.

### 🇨🇱 Chile
The Chilean Senate approved the corporate tax reduction at the heart of President Kast's mega-reform — bringing the rate from 27% to 23% by 2029 — by a razor-thin 26-to-24 margin, a vote JP Morgan called "the most important political catalyst for Chilean equities this year" in a report accompanying its recent upgrade of Chilean stocks to Overweight. The legislative win coexists with a Budget Office report showing the structural fiscal gap does not close in any year through 2030, with the structural deficit projected at 2.4% of GDP in 2027 against a 1.8% target, while Clapes UC puts the probability of a technical recession at 13% after five consecutive months of Imacec declines. The text must still clear the Chamber of Deputies, where constitutional reservations filed by at least two senators add legal uncertainty that could stretch resolution into the autumn.

### 🇺🇾 Uruguay
The IMF managing director's visit to Montevideo — where she met with President Orsi, Finance Minister Oddone, and the central bank governor — carried explicit pressure for greater fiscal consolidation at a moment when the government itself concedes that its 2026 growth forecast faces a likely downward revision. The fiscal deficit closed 2025 at 4.1% of GDP, the World Bank has trimmed its 2026 projection to 1.6%, and the Autonomous Fiscal Council warned Parliament that the Ministry of Economy's own growth assumptions underpinning the Rendición de Cuentas are too optimistic. Technology services exports reaching an all-time high and a record wheat harvest offer genuine bright spots, but the gap between Uruguay's institutional reputation and its current growth dynamism is the central tension that Oddone will need to resolve — in Parliament and before the IMF — in the weeks ahead.

Link: https://24econews.com/uruguay/2026-07-16

### 🇵🇾 Paraguay
Paraguay's economy expanded 5.6% through May and the central bank recorded first-quarter growth of 5.8%, figures that lead the region — and that President Peña leaned on heavily in his state-of-the-nation address. What Peña omitted is equally significant: the government is processing more than US$1.6 billion in new external credits while preparing a fresh sovereign bond issue, interest payments on public debt grew 12.9% in the most recent period, the Caja Fiscal is running a five-month deficit of US$182 million, and customs revenues are falling as the exchange rate shifts. The reform of the Caja Fiscal — whose approval Chamber president Llanes Alliana has committed to before week's end — and seven additional economic bills from the new Economy Ministry under Óscar Lovera will be the near-term tests of whether Paraguay's institutional framework can translate its growth momentum into durable fiscal sustainability.

### 🇧🇴 Bolivia
Bolivia absorbed three simultaneous shocks on a single trading day: the first unrestricted dollar withdrawals from banks — covering more than US$930 million in previously frozen deposits — began under the central bank's staggered schedule; the flexible exchange rate introduced on June 29 moved from Bs 10.40 to Bs 10.50 within 24 hours, with Economy Minister Espinoza targeting stabilization below Bs 11, implying a cumulative depreciation of more than 58% from the old peg of Bs 6.96; and the government officially confirmed that diesel and gasoline shortages will persist through the end of July, even as it froze fuel prices for another six months to January 2027, preserving the subsidy that Fitch has identified as one of the region's heaviest fiscal burdens. Cochabamba's private sector federation projects a 4.15% economic contraction for the department in 2026, and the minister himself acknowledged that national GDP could turn negative this year after 47 days of blockades that cost an estimated US$2.7 billion.

## Corporate Watch of the Week

**Ânima Educação (B3: ANIM3) — Brazil:** Shares collapsed 32.75% in a single session after the company announced the acquisition of FMU for R$410 million, erasing R$380 million in market value. BTG, Itaú BBA, J.P. Morgan, Citi, Bradesco BBI, and Jefferies collectively criticized the transaction as expensive for a company at its current valuation multiple — a rare instance of near-universal sell-side dissent that signals deep governance concerns in Brazil's private education sector.

**BHP Group (NYSE: BHP) — Chile:** The world's largest miner reported copper production of 1.95 million tonnes in fiscal 2026, just below the two-million-tonne threshold for the second consecutive year, with Escondida contributing 1.2 million tonnes in a year when copper prices ran roughly 35% above the prior year. The forward guidance is the more market-relevant data point: BHP projects output declining to between 1.65 and 1.8 million tonnes in fiscal 2027 on expected grade deterioration at Escondida, introducing a supply constraint at the precise moment Chile's fiscal arithmetic depends on sustained copper revenues.

**Petrobras (NYSE: PBR) — Bolivia/Brazil:** Bolivia and Brazil agreed to establish technical working groups to expand Petrobras's participation in the Bolivian hydrocarbons sector, against a backdrop of YPFB output at three-decade lows. For Petrobras — already managing the fallout from the US tariff shock on its ADR exposure — the agreement represents a potential new production and export contract that could partially offset Bolivia's domestic supply collapse, though concrete timelines remain unconfirmed and the country's currency volatility adds execution risk to any near-term investment commitment.

## What to Watch Next Week

- **Brazil's retaliation timeline and tariff negotiations with Washington:** The Lula government has invoked the Reciprocity Law and is designing countermeasures, while the Trump administration has signaled the 25% rate could move in either direction depending on Brasília's response. The shape and speed of Brazil's formal reply — including any WTO filings — will be the single most important market-moving variable across the region next week. National Treasury fixed-rate bond auctions will simultaneously test whether domestic investors are willing to absorb local risk at current yield levels amid the accumulated pressure of the tariff, revised inflation, and a presidential race that is now feeding into the long end of the yield curve.

- **Bolivia's exchange rate and dollar withdrawal dynamics:** With unrestricted bank dollar withdrawals now underway and the flexible exchange rate still seeking its floor, the central bank's capacity to hold the boliviano below the psychologically critical Bs 11 threshold will be closely monitored. Any intervention — or failure to intervene — will signal how much firepower La Paz has left and whether the Petrobras technical working group agreement can be formalized quickly enough to restore private sector confidence before the business summit turns adversarial.

- **Chile's Chamber of Deputies vote on the mega-reform and Paraguay's Caja Fiscal legislation:** Both countries face pivotal legislative moments next week. In Santiago, the Chamber must ratify a Senate-passed corporate tax reform that cleared by a two-vote margin and faces constitutional challenges from at least two senators. In Asunción, the Chamber president has committed to approving Caja Fiscal reform before the week ends — a promise whose delivery will determine whether Paraguay's fiscal credibility holds as sovereign debt costs continue to climb.
